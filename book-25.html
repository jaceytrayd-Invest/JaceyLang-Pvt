The Folio · Book 25

Trophimus Have I Left

On the soul that remained unfinished, unresolved, and yet still belonged to God

A prophetic series in ten parts — drawn from 2 Timothy 4:20 — on the soul that discovers that not every faithful servant is granted immediate resolution; and on the terrible holiness of continuing to belong to God while something remains painfully unfinished.

Prologue

The Sentence That Remained Open

It is one of the loneliest sentences in Scripture.
No miracle surrounds it. No explanation steadies it. No ending softens it.
Only this:
“Trophimus have I left…”
And then —
“sick.”
The letter moves onward, yet the weakness remains standing behind it.
Paul did not need to mention him.
The sentence changes nothing doctrinally. Adds nothing strategically. Solves nothing practically.
Yet love carried the wounded man into the epistle.
As though the apostle could not continue speaking without first allowing the sorrow to breathe once upon the page.
O soul — some affections are so deeply joined in God that even unresolved suffering refuses to disappear silently from the heart.
Chapter I

Left Behind

The sorrow was not merely sickness.
It was remaining behind.
Paul continued onward. The work continued onward. The kingdom continued onward.
Yet Trophimus remained where weakness had overtaken him.
And Scripture does not rush to close the distance between them.
O soul — the deepest grief is often not affliction itself, but watching life continue onward while thy own condition remains unresolved.
Chapter II

The Boundary of Love

This was Paul.
The apostle through whom the dead had risen. The man through whom mercy had flowed into afflicted places. The servant who had once carried strength into the weakness of others.
Yet here —
he leaves a beloved companion sick.
And suddenly all triumph grows quieter.
For even holy love sometimes reaches the place where it can no longer remove the suffering of the one it carries.
O soul — there are griefs before which even the most faithful love must stand helpless and still continue loving.
Chapter III

The Prison and the Distance

And Paul himself was no longer free.
The letter came from confinement. From narrowing roads. From the slow closing of earthly ministry.
Perhaps that is partly why the sentence aches so deeply.
The apostle who once crossed nations freely could now do little more than remember.
Unable to return. Unable to carry the burden away. Unable perhaps even to know how the sickness now fared.
Yet still —
he spoke the name.
O soul — there are seasons when love can no longer reach through action, and must continue only through remembrance, sorrow, and prayer.
Chapter IV

The Silence of Heaven

No reason is given.
That is what unsettles the mind.
Had Scripture explained the purpose, the soul could rest within understanding.
But heaven leaves the matter open.
And silence is harder to bear than explanation.
For silence requires the soul to trust the goodness of God where nothing has yet been resolved.
O soul — the Lord sometimes leaves His deepest workings unexplained, lest thy faith begin depending upon answers rather than upon Him.
Chapter V

The Unfinished Condition

The sickness remained.
Morning after morning. Night after night.
Not dramatic enough to end the story — yet not lifted enough to restore strength.
And perhaps this became the deeper burden:
not crisis —
but continuance.
The weary repetition of remaining unfinished before God.
O soul — some afflictions wound most deeply not through their violence, but through their long refusal to conclude.
Chapter VI

The Temptation Beneath Delay

There comes a hidden temptation in prolonged unresolvedness:
to quietly interpret delay as distance from God.
To imagine that what remains unhealed must somehow remain untouched by divine love.
Yet Trophimus was not forgotten.
The unfinished condition was not proof of abandonment.
For God sometimes remains nearest where His purposes remain least explained.
O soul — do not measure the nearness of God by the speed with which He removes thy burden.
Chapter VII

The Fellowship of the Unresolved

Scripture leaves Trophimus standing among a hidden company:
those who remained faithful without visible conclusion.
Not rebels. Not forsaken men.
Only souls whom God did not fully explain before the chapter closed.
And heaven did not count them lesser.
Perhaps future generations needed this sentence preserved —
not to explain suffering, but to reveal a love that continued carrying another even where healing had not come.
O soul — the kingdom contains many whom God has deeply loved while still leaving portions of their earthly story unresolved.
Chapter VIII

The Continuance of Love

Perhaps the greater wonder was not healing.
Perhaps it was this:
that neither weakness nor distance appears to have severed affection.
The work continued. The imprisonments continued. The suffering continued.
Yet somewhere within the apostle, the wounded companion still remained carried.
And perhaps heaven preserved that sigh deliberately.
For ministry was never meant to be transaction.
Not usefulness. Not outcomes. Not merely victories recorded.
But love that refuses to forget even where it cannot repair.
O soul — the purest love is often revealed not in what it succeeds in healing, but in what it faithfully continues carrying before God.
Chapter IX

The Final Surrender

Perhaps the holiest moment comes when the soul ceases demanding that every sorrow be visibly resolved.
When it no longer says:
“Explain Thyself.”
Only:
“Keep me near Thee even here.”
And there, beneath unfinished things, a quieter faith begins forming —
one no longer sustained by outcomes.
O soul — maturity before God begins when the soul learns to remain yielded even where nothing has yet been resolved.
Chapter X

The Summons

Wilt thou continue loving God where explanations fail?
Wilt thou remain near Him where relief delays?
Wilt thou still belong to Him even where thy story remains unfinished before men?
And wilt thou still love others faithfully even where thou canst not heal, rescue, or restore them?

O soul — blessed is the man who discovers that God Himself remains sufficient even where earthly resolution never arrives.
Closing Poem

Trophimus at Miletum

T R O P H I M U S   A T   M I L E T U M
One sentence lingered in the scroll, Though many names passed by: “Trophimus have I left behind,” And no clear reason why.
No mighty wonder closed the wound, No sudden healing came; The weakness simply still remained, Unlifted all the same.
The apostle journeyed slowly on, Yet sorrow travelled too; For love could not forget the one Whom suffering still knew.
And from a prison’s narrowing walls His heart still reached that place; Though hands could not remove the grief, Love would not lose the face.
Thus heaven kept the sigh preserved Where no full answer stood; To teach that love may yet remain Where none can alter good.
O Lord, when life stays unresolved And burdens still remain, Keep me both near Thee and near those Who suffer long in pain.

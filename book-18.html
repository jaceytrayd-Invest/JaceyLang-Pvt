The Folio · Book 18

O My Son Absalom

On the soul that must submit to the will of God, yet cannot cease to love what He has taken

A prophetic series in six chapters — drawn from 2 Samuel 18–19 — on David, who was upheld in the purpose of God, yet undone by the loss of his son; on the victory that brought him no joy; and on the grief that remained, though the kingdom stood.

Prologue

The Charge That Revealed the Heart

“Deal gently.”
He spoke it — not as king, but as father.
Before the battle was joined, his heart was already divided.
The cause was set. The judgment stood.
Yet love spoke otherwise.
O soul — thou mayest stand in the will of God,
and yet plead against what it will require.
Chapter I

The Battle and the Son Taken

The field was set. Men moved. Swords answered.
The matter passed from him.
He could command — but not govern the end.
O soul — there are outcomes in God’s dealings
that pass beyond thy reach,
though they concern thee most.
It was done.
Not gently. Not as he had desired.
The young man hung — caught, exposed. And the blow fell.
No voice remained to answer him.
O soul — what God permits
may not regard the tenderness
thou wouldst have preserved.
They ran. With news of victory — and of death.
The kingdom stood. The enemy was scattered.
Yet the word he waited for was not the triumph.
O soul — thou mayest receive what is right,
and yet seek only what is lost.
Chapter II

The Cry That Broke the Order

“O my son Absalom…”
It came forth — not measured, not restrained.
“My son, my son…”
The king vanished. The father remained.
“Would God I had died for thee…”
The will of God stood — yet his heart refused it.
O soul — there is a grief that rises,
even where God has judged.
Chapter III

The Victory Turned to Mourning

The people returned. Not with gladness — but in shame.
Victory was swallowed. Triumph silenced. For the king wept.
O soul — what God has established outwardly
may be overshadowed
by what breaks thee inwardly.
They stole away. Not openly — but as men ashamed.
The deliverance of many was made small by the sorrow of one.
O soul — when grief governs thee,
it may touch more than thyself.
Chapter IV

The Word and the Rising

Then came the word. Not gentle. Not spared.
Thou lovest thine enemies, and hatest thy friends.
The truth stood — hard, unyielding.
O soul — God may send a word
that does not comfort thy grief,
but exposes it.
He arose.
Not because he was restored — but because he must.
He took his place. The king returned — yet not the man.
O soul — there is a rising that comes
not from healing,
but from necessity.
The throne was his. The kingdom endured. Nothing was lost — save one.
Yet that one remained all.
O soul — thou mayest retain all that God has given,
and yet be held by what He has taken.
He did not resist. He did not turn aside. He continued.
Yet within — no agreement was found.
The will of God stood. The heart endured it.
O soul — there is a submission that does not resolve the soul,
but binds it to continue.
Chapter V

The Summons

Wilt thou yield what God has judged?
Wilt thou walk on when thy heart remains behind?
Wilt thou serve when love is not answered?

O soul — the will of God may stand,
though thy heart cannot follow it.
Chapter VI

O My Son Absalom

O   M Y   S O N   A B S A L O M
He spake before the battle joined, “Deal gently with my son”; Yet judgment moved without regard, And all was quickly done.
The tidings came of victory, The kingdom stood secure; Yet one was lost, and in that loss No triumph could endure.
“O Absalom, my son, my son, Would God I died for thee”; The will of God remained unchanged, Yet grief would not agree.
The people turned their joy to shame, And stole away from sight; For sorrow sat upon the throne And dimmed the day of might.
O Lord, when Thou hast judged aright,
Yet love would still remain;
Give us to walk beneath Thy hand,
Though wounded by the same.

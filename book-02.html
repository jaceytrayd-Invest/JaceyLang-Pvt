All Asia Turned Away

…and the Soul Found All in God

A devotional work in five chapters — on forsaking, suffering, and the grace that leads the soul into God alone.

Chapter I

Historical and Scriptural Context

The “Asia” here spoken of is not the vast continent as now known, but the Roman province of Asia— lying within what is present-day western Turkey— among whom the apostle Paul faithfully laboured.
In his letters to the churches there, he records a season of profound forsaking. Not a moment of failure, but the archetype of every true calling.
“You know that all who are in Asia have turned away from me.”
— 2 Timothy 1:15
“At my first defence, no man stood with me,
but the Lord stood by me.”
— 2 Timothy 4:16–17
“Since Christ suffered in the flesh,
arm yourselves with the same mind…
that ye may live no longer to the lusts of men,
but to the will of God.”
— 1 Peter 4:1–2
This moment of turning away was no mere personal trial. It is the archetype of the path of every true calling— a season in which the familiar supports of life are withdrawn, that the soul may stand wholly upon the Lord.
Chapter II

Devotional Framing

There is a deeper work of grace, whereby the Lord, in wisdom, detaches the soul from every created stay, that He alone may be its life and its rest.
This work is not accomplished at once, nor without suffering. The heart, long accustomed to lean upon visible things— upon brethren, upon cherished companions, upon the felt nearness of human love— must be weaned.
And this weaning is wrought often by a series of removals, each touching some tender affection. Yet in all this there is no severity but love. That which He takes away, He does so in counsel; that which He removes, He replenishes with that which is unseen yet abiding.
He does not impoverish the soul, but enriches it with Himself. Thus the believer is led, step by step, into that state where Christ is not merely a help, nor the chief among many consolations, but the one sufficient portion of the heart.
Herein lies the calling to be “armed in suffering”—
not to endure only,
but to receive in it the mind of Christ,
and to be separated, in will and desire,
unto God alone.
He does not lead the soul through darkness because He is absent from it, but because He would be more fully known within it. Every removal is a preparation. Every stripping is a filling. Every wound is a door.
Chapter III

The Sonnets

1.   A S I A   T U R N E D
All Asia turned; the faces once my stay Withdrew their warmth and left the pathway bare. I felt the sudden chill of love’s decay— Then knew: this too was held within His care.
At Ephesus I bore their griefs as mine; Their lives I pressed like fruit against my breast. When they fell back, I traced the hidden sign: The hand that wounds the flesh prepares its rest.
Thus suffering in flesh from brethren dear Unbound my will from all that once was sweet. Their absence made the Lord more sharply near, And loss became the place where wills could meet.
So fleshly ties released me into Him: Their turning loosed my soul from every limb.
2.   T H E   S O N   N O T   S E N T
I called thy name through many quiet hours; Thy tears still lived, remembered in my own. I thought to see thee ere the failing powers— Yet winter closed, and I remained alone.
No blame was thine; love’s bond was never cleft. But hope in flesh, though pure, was laid aside. And in the space thy nearness would have left, The Lord Himself stood living at my side.
Thus suffering want of what my heart desired Drew every thread of will from man to God. What once I sought grew gentle, then expired; His presence filled the room thy feet had trod.
Not less my son—yet sweeter still to see: Christ came instead, and was enough for me.
3.   T H E   L O R D   S T O O D   N E A R
No man stood by me; all withdrew from sight. I felt the nakedness of faith alone. Yet in that stripping came a fuller Light: The Lord stood near, and claimed me as His own.
The last support of human nearness fell; No voice remained to mingle with my breath. Then rose within the hush Emmanuel— More close than life, more intimate than death.
Thus armed in suffering from fleshly stay, My will grew still toward all that men intend. To live for God alone became my way; His will, once hard, grew tender as a friend.
Let flesh fall back, and earthly comforts cease: God’s will embraced becomes the soul’s deep peace.
4.   T H E   W I L L   A T   R E S T
When all was stripped that once had seemed as life, And every voice of flesh had fallen still, There rose no longing, grief, nor inward strife— But quiet union of the yielded will.
No more I reached for what was once my stay; No shadow stirred to draw my heart abroad. For in the loss that took all else away, My soul had learned the single will of God.
Not by resolve, nor strength of inward frame, But through the wounds that love had seen to give, The restless “I” grew tender, then grew tame, And ceased from self, that Christ alone might live.
Thus armed in suffering, the conflict ceased: The will in God, and God the will—at peace.
Chapter IV

Exhortation

Beloved, take heart in seasons of removal and loneliness. Every calling bears its “Asia moment”— a time when the familiar supports fail, when companionship is withdrawn, and the path grows solitary.
Do not seek relief in what has been taken away. Let the absence drive your heart deeper into Christ. Here, in the quiet and the stripping, He becomes your all-sufficient portion.
Learn to rest not in companions, favour, or comfort,
but in the presence of the Lord Himself.
What is taken away is never lost— it is transformed into a channel of blessing, preparing you to strengthen others in ways that human presence cannot achieve.
Chapter V

Epilogue

Thus it was in the closing path of Paul the Apostle. Those in Asia turned away. Timothy was not at hand. At his first defence no man stood with him. All outward stay failed together.
Yet this stripping was not loss, but enlargement. For every true calling, there comes an “Asia moment”— a season when the familiar support withdraws, the path grows lonely, and the soul is compelled to stand wholly upon the Lord.
It is in this very absence that the believer is armed in suffering, and that Christ alone becomes sufficient. For in the absence of all, the Lord stands near in ways unknown before.
What had once been enjoyed in part is now possessed in fullness. Christ is no longer one consolation among many, but the very life of the soul.
In those final years, though bound in chains, Paul’s ministry did not diminish. Rather, it was purified. What was wrought in suffering became a hidden fountain of strength for many. His testimony, born in solitude, has breathed courage into multitudes.
Thus the Lord permits the servant to be emptied, that he may be filled; to be shut in, that life may flow out more freely.
And the soul that has learned Christ in this way
rests, not in circumstance, nor in human aid,
but in God alone.

The Folio · Book 17

Doest Thou Well to Be Angry?

On the soul that has obeyed, endured, and yet cannot agree with God

A prophetic series in six chapters — drawn from Jonah 4 — on the man who fulfilled the command laid upon him, yet found no rest in its outcome; on the mercy of God that undid him; and on the question that remained unanswered within him.

Prologue

The Work Completed

It was done.
The word had gone forth. The city had heard.
Nothing remained undone.
What was required of him he had fulfilled.
O soul — there is an obedience that reaches its end,
and finds no peace waiting there.
God turned.
The judgment did not fall. The word did not end as spoken.
What had been declared was withheld.
O soul — God may act beyond His word,
and overturn what thou hast carried.
Chapter I

The Displeasure

It displeased him.
Not slightly. Not in passing.
Exceedingly.
He was not uncertain. He was not confused. He was offended.
O soul — there is a state
wherein thou dost not question God,
but resist Him.
“I knew.”
Not learned now — but known before.
That Thou art gracious. That Thou repentest of evil.
What was revealed became the cause of his anger.
O soul — what thou knowest of God
may rise against Him within thee.
He spoke.
Not to align — but to depart.
“Take, I beseech Thee, my life.”
Not from despair alone — but from refusal.
O soul — there is a prayer that does not seek God,
but release from Him.
Chapter II

The Question Given

“Doest thou well to be angry?”
It came. Not with force. Not with judgment.
A question — laid upon him.
He was not corrected. He was addressed.
O soul — God may meet thy resistance,
and not remove it.
He went out.
Not resolved. Not yielded.
He left the place of God’s working, and sat apart.
The question remained — unanswered within him.
O soul — thou mayest withdraw,
while still carrying what God has spoken.
Chapter III

The Shelter and the Mercy

He made a booth.
Not given — but formed.
A covering of his own making, to endure what he would not accept.
He sat beneath it — waiting still.
O soul — thou mayest build for thyself what God has not provided,
to remain where thou refusest to yield.
The Lord prepared a gourd.
It rose over him. It covered his head.
A small mercy — given without request.
And he was glad.
O soul — God may give thee comfort,
though thou art not right before Him.
Then it was taken.
Not by chance — but appointed.
The covering withered. The shadow fled. The sun beat down.
O soul — what God gives He may remove,
not in anger, but in purpose.
Nothing stood between him and the heat. No shelter remained.
What had been hidden was now laid bare.
His gladness had been shallow. His endurance — dependent.
O soul — God may strip from thee what sustains thee,
to show thee what thou art.
Chapter IV

The Anger Justified

“I do well to be angry.”
He held it. Not confessed. Not softened.
Even unto death. He did not yield.
O soul — there is a hardness that can remain,
even after God has spoken.
Thou hast had pity on the gourd.
For which thou hast not laboured. Which came in a night, and perished in a night.
And should not I spare Nineveh?
The measure was shown. His heart — set against God’s.
O soul — God may reveal thy heart,
and yet leave thee to answer.
No reply is given. No yielding recorded. No turning shown.
The question remains — open.

O soul — there are dealings of God that end without resolution written.
Chapter V

The Summons

Wilt thou obey — yet resist?
Wilt thou endure — yet oppose?
Wilt thou know God — yet refuse His ways?

O soul — the deepest fracture is not when thou canst not find God,
but when thou canst not agree with Him.
Chapter VI

Doest Thou Well

D O E S T   T H O U   W E L L
The word was borne, the city turned, The threatened wrath withdrew; Yet he who spake the warning forth No rest within him knew.
He knew the Lord was full of grace, And slow to anger still; Yet mercy shown to those he warned Opposed his secret will.
He left the place where God had wrought, And sat himself apart; A shelter made by his own hand To house an unquiet heart.
A gourd was given, then removed, A shadow briefly cast; Till all that eased his outward state Was taken from him fast.
“I do well,” thus his anger stood, And would not be denied; While God set forth the greater good He could not justify.
O Lord, when we have done Thy will,
Yet find our hearts at strife;
Subdue in us what wars with Thee,
And bring our souls to life.

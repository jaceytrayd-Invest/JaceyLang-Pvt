Alas, Master —
For It Is Borrowed

2 Kings 6:5 — "Alas, master! for it was borrowed."

A prophetic series in sixteen chapters — on stewardship, borrowed authority, and the mercy that lifts what sank.

Opening

The Cry Before the Series

There is a kind of loss that does not announce itself when it begins.No noise. No rupture. No visible fall.Just a slow, quiet leaking of something you once held with fear.
And you don't notice it immediately.You keep moving. Keep speaking.Keep responding the way you always have.Nothing looks broken. Nothing seems gone.
Until one moment— not loud, not dramatic— just… undeniable.Something is not where it used to be.Not gone entirely. But not whole either.
And suddenly, without preparation, without polished language— you understand him.In Bible Second Kings 6:5, a man does not preach.He does not explain. He cries.
"Alas, master! for it was borrowed."
That cry is not theology. It is shock colliding with truth.It is the sound of a man who just realized two things at once—he has lost something, and he had no right to lose it.
Because what fell into the Jordan River was not just iron.It was pretense.
Somewhere before the fall, before the slip,before the sound of it vanishing beneath the surface—he had grown used to holding it. Too used to it.
Borrowed things, over time, begin to feel like they belong to you.You stop checking. Stop fearing.Stop handling them like they could be taken.
Until they are.
And the river is merciless in that way.It does not expose you gradually.It swallows everything at once— the object, the illusion,the quiet pride that came with it.
Now there is no room for performance.No space to explain.No language strong enough to recover dignity.Only a cry. Raw. Unfiltered. Almost embarrassing in its honesty:
"Alas…"
Because what do you say when you didn't walk away from God—but something of Him is no longer resting on you like it once did?What do you call it when prayer still exists, but weight is missing?When truth still sounds right, but does not cut as deep?When sin is still avoided— but no longer mourned?
This is not open rebellion.This is something harder to detect.A thinning. A quiet drifting into handling holy things without trembling.
And one day— you feel it.Not fully gone. But not fully there.And in that moment, you are no longer reading that passage.You are standing in it. Handle in hand. Heart sinking. No explanation left.
This series comes from that place.Not from strength. Not from clarity.But from the edge of realization—where something has slipped, and you cannot pretend you didn't feel it go.
It will not rush you to comfort.It will not quickly restore you.It will not let you hide behind language.It will walk you down to the water and leave you there long enough to tell the truth.
Because before anything can be lifted— before mercy moves—before anything impossible happens—there must be a cry that is finally honest:
"It was borrowed."
Part I

Borrowed, Not Owned

Alas, master.
Not "my axe head." Not "something misplaced." Not "what I earned."It was borrowed. And that is the first knife.The one that cuts deep because it is truth.
Once upon a time, he might have pretended it was his.A little lie. A touch of pride.A swinging of borrowed authority as if the weight in his hand proved possession.But the Jordan River swallowed it all.
The iron sank. And with it: the ego. The pretense.The quiet arrogance that whispered, "I can hold this forever."
He did not shout. He did not calculate.He did not argue with the river.He just wailed. Howled. Cried like a man whose language is caught in his throat.Because there is nothing you can say when the sacred is gone.When what was lent to you is no longer resting on your hands.
And yet— there is a faint trace.A thin mercy still moving in the moment: The tree.The one that will carry him long before he knows its name.Long before its branches will lift the world.
He clings to the prophet. Desperate. Exposed.Knowing nothing but faith can intervene.He does not know how it will return.He does not know if it will rise.He only knows he cannot retrieve it alone.
Because iron cannot float.Weight cannot hang in midair by effort.Power cannot linger where it is not given.
And this is the terror of stewardship:You can hold the handle. You can swing the motion. You can keep the rhythm.But if God's presence lifts, it becomes a hidden betrayal.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.

Not sorrow for loss. Not complaint for inconvenience.
Not longing for return. Mortification.
Because if it were yours— you could recover it.If it were yours— you could sharpen it again.If it were yours— effort might restore it. But it wasn't.
And now he is left with nothing but the echo of what he had:The handle. The memory of weight.The tremor of fear. The first, desperate, gasping cry:
"Alas, master… it was borrowed."
And somewhere in that wail, he begins to see it:The mercy is coming.Not because he deserves it. Not because he earned it.But because God is merciful.
The axe may have sunk. The river may have swallowed it. But the tree is there.And the mercy of God waits,even for those who have handled the sacred carelessly.
"It was never mine, but You still see me."
Part II

The Slip You Didn't Feel

It didn't begin with the fall.Not with the splash of iron.Not with the gasp of the river swallowing what was lent.It began somewhere quieter. Somewhere almost invisible.Where the handle was still in your hand,and you didn't notice the weight shifting.
A moment of distraction. A pause in vigilance.A thought: "It will always be here. I've carried it long enough."
That is where the betrayal starts.Not loud. Not scandalous. Not even sinful in the obvious sense.But deadly.
Because spiritual things do not hover.They do not wait politely. They do not tolerate familiarity.They sink.
He kept swinging. The motion was familiar.From the outside, nothing seemed changed.But inside, something was gone.The edge dulled. The presence thinned. The sensitivity waned.And you did not feel it. Not yet.
You still prayed. You still responded. You still avoided what was wrong.But the river was already moving, already taking,already exposing what you had quietly begun to treat as yours.
And the terror is in that invisibility:You can carry what is empty and no one will see it.You can speak with memory and still sound like truth.You can continue in service and still be hollow.
The slip is the quiet collapse.The unnoticed abandonment of what you were entrusted to guard.
And then one day— you feel it.A lightness. A thinness. A faint panic that rises without warning.The handle is still there. The motion continues. But the weight is gone.
And for the first time, you realize the depth of what you ignored:That which was borrowed cannot be treated as yours.
The river has already done its work.The water has taken the iron. The pretense has gone under.And you are left with the tremor in your chest:The slip you didn't feel. The moment that set everything in motion.
"Alas, master… it was borrowed."
Because now you know: It was never just about losing an object.It was about losing vigilance. Losing fear. Losing faithfulness.And you cannot retrieve that by yourself.The only hope is mercy. The only path back is the One who sees.
The tree is still there, waiting.Even for those who only now feel the slip.Even for those who only now begin to wail.Even for those who only now see the betrayal in their hands.
Part III

When Iron Sinks

Iron does not float.Not in water. Not in heaven. Not in the spirit.
This is the hard truth the sons of the prophets could not soften:Whatever is neglected, whatever is treated as yours instead of borrowed,descends. It sinks quietly. It sinks fast. It sinks without asking your permission.
The axe head did not argue. It did not fight.It simply obeyed the law of its nature:Weight cannot stay suspended where it is not held by God.
And neither can spiritual things.Presence. Sensitivity. Faithful testimony.A good conscience. Trust from others.All of it sinks if you fail as a steward.
You keep moving. You keep swinging. You keep performing.But what once carried weight no longer does.
And the horror is this:People still trust you. People still believe you are sharp.People still rely on the rhythm of your swing.All the while, the iron has gone under. And you know it.
The river takes no excuses. It swallows the ego along with the edge.It unmasks the small lie:You thought this was yours. You thought it would always be there.
There is no ceremony in sinking.No judgment pronounced. No dramatic trumpet.Just silence. Just absence.Just the weight that is no longer resting on your hands.
And in that silence, you realize:You were never carrying iron.You were carrying memory. You were swinging empty.
Alas, master. "It was borrowed."
And yet— there is still mercy.The tree is waiting. The cross has already been cast in place.The river cannot undo what God's mercy will lift.But first… you must watch the iron sink.You must feel the weight leave.You must face the terrifying truth that what you held was never yours to keep.
Only then can the impossible begin:Iron rising. Presence restored. Faithfulness returned.Not by effort. Not by pride. Not by pretense.But by God alone.
Part IV

Swinging Without Edge

And here is the cruelest part: You keep swinging.The handle is in your hand. The motion is familiar.From the outside, nothing seems wrong.But inside… The edge is gone. The weight is gone. The presence is gone.
You continue as if nothing has changed.You speak with confidence. You respond with authority. You minister, advise, lead.All the while, what once made your words sharpand your actions heavy has sunk beneath the water.
And the terror is subtle:No one can see it. No one can measure it.No one can confront it— except the One who lent it to you in the first place.
You are trusted because of memory.Because of what once rested on you.Because of the edge you used to carry.
And that is betrayal in the quietest sense.Not rebellion. Not open sin.But emptiness masquerading as presence.Form without power. Motion without substance. Speech without presence.
"Alas… master… it was borrowed."
You are standing at the river,swinging a handle that carries no iron,and the world cannot tell the difference.Only God sees. Only the prophet sees. Only your conscience knows.
This is the danger of familiarity:To continue in motion while the essential has left.To believe you are faithful when you are only imitating faithfulness.
And yet— there is still a thin thread of hope.Because the tree waits. The mercy waits. The impossible waits.But first, you must see yourself swinging without edge.First, you must wail. First, you must recognize:You cannot restore what was never yours.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.And until you truly face that, the swing is empty.The words are hollow.The ministry is a shadow of what God intended.
Part V

The Betrayal No One Sees

This is the cruelest betrayal:Not the river taking the iron. Not the slip unnoticed.Not even the dulling of the edge.No. The worst betrayal is what others believe is still there.
They trust you. They follow your counsel. They rely on your presence.All the while, you know— deep down—Something essential has left your hands.Something sacred has slipped from your grip.And yet, from their eyes, you appear untouched.
This is the terror of stewardship:You are holding what is no longer yours,and everyone assumes you are still faithful.They believe in the weight that once rested on you.They rely on the fire that once burned in your spirit.They see a motion that looks like ministry.But inside… The iron is gone. The edge is gone. The presence is gone.
And here is where shame becomes sharp:Because you cannot confess it easily.Because admitting it exposes more than failure.Because the world cannot see what God already knows.
You are carrying trust without the substance to sustain it.You are walking in memory while heaven measures reality.You are performing in form while substance has left.
And yet… Even in the terror, even in the guilt,there is a fragile thread:The prophet is still there. Mercy is still there.The tree waits in the water, ready to lift what sank.
But first… You must face the betrayal no one sees.You must see it in yourself before mercy can move.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.
And until you reckon with this hidden betrayal, you cannot move forward.You cannot recover.You cannot truly speak with the weight you once held.
Only when the cry becomes honest—"It was borrowed, and I have failed as a steward"—Only then can mercy find a place to act.
Part VI

Dim Light and False Normalcy

There comes a season where you move in dim light.Not darkness. Not full night. Just dim.Enough to keep going. Enough to perform.Enough to convince yourself you are fine.
And for a while, you are.You are functional. You are seen as faithful.You are trusted. You are even respected.But inside… Something is off. Something is missing.A weight that once cut through every moment is gone.
This is the danger of false normalcy:You no longer tremble at sin.You no longer grieve what is wrong.You no longer feel the holy pressurethat once kept your hands careful and your steps aligned.
You are still moving,but the river has already taken what gave your motion its edge.You can preach. You can pray. You can respond.But you are dimmer. You are thinner.You are no longer the man who trembled at God's presence.
And that is the subtle, crushing terror:You have drifted into entitlement disguised as routine.You handle what was borrowed as if it were permanently yours.You convince yourself that everything is fine,while heaven quietly counts what you have lost.
But even here, mercy waits.The tree is still in the water. The cross is still coming.The One who lent the weight is still able to restore what sank.
Yet first, you must recognize:The dim light is not normal. The ease is not safety.The absence of grief is not peace.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.
And until you feel the hollow, until you recognize the false normalcy,you will continue swinging in form—while the edge remains at the river bottom.
Only the cry that admits "I am dim, I am careless, I am failing"—Only that cry opens a place for mercy to act.
Part VII

The Shock of Realization

Then the light returns.Not slowly, not gently. But in a way that blinds you.It hits like a sudden sun after a long dim winter.And everything you thought was steady, solid, faithful, begins to tremble.
You see the truth of your hands. The truth of your swing. The truth of your motion.And it terrifies you.Because for the first time, you realize:You have been living in memory. You have been carrying shadows.You have been trusted for what is no longer present in you.
The shock is sharp:The grace you once handled with trembling has slipped.The edge you once wielded is dulled.The presence you once carried is thinner than you imagined.And with this realization comes the cry, broken and raw:
"Alas, master… it was borrowed!"

Not as a teaching. Not as a confession to be praised.
But as the desperate gasp of a man facing what he allowed to sink.
It is a holy terror:To see that what you assumed was yours was never yours.To see that what others still trust you for is only what memory can imitate.To see that the motion of ministry, service, and lifeis no longer empowered by what was lent.
And in that shock, two things happen:1. The illusion of entitlement dies.2. The necessity of mercy becomes undeniable.
You cannot explain it away. You cannot recover it alone.You cannot pretend it is fine.The river has shown the truth.The tree is the only hope. The prophet is the only guide.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.And until you face the shock, you will continue swinging—but without the weight, the edge, the power that once made your motion holy.
Part VIII

Entitlement in Holy Things

Here is the sin that hides beneath all others: Entitlement.Not rebellion. Not open defiance.Not the kind of failure that shouts for attention.No. This is subtler. Deadlier.The quiet arrogance that whispers:
"It is mine to carry.
It will never be taken.
I earned this. I deserve this."
You begin to treat what is borrowed as if it were permanent.You handle presence carelessly.You speak with authority you did not check.You move without trembling where once you trembled.And all the while, heaven is waiting for faithfulness.
But you… You are comfortable. You are confident. You are entitled.And that entitlement sinks the iron faster than any river.
It is the betrayal that cuts deepest:You do not realize you are failing.You do not feel the erosion inside your soul.You convince yourself you are faithfulwhile the weight, the edge, the life-giving presence is quietly slipping away.
And the moment you notice— if you notice at all—The axe is gone. The river has swallowed the proof.The little lie you carried about ownership has been exposed.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.
Not yours to claim. Not yours to swing. Not yours to trust in.
And yet, mercy waits.The tree is still in the river. The cross still carries hope.The One who lent the iron is still able to lift it again.But first… You must confess the entitlement.You must feel the shame. You must wail the cry:
"It was borrowed, and I treated it as mine."
Only then can what sank begin to rise.Only then can mercy enter where pride once ruled.
Part IX

Standing at the Water's Edge

And now— you are here.Handle in hand. Heart racing. Breath caught.The river before you is calm.The water reflects nothing of your pretense.Only the absence. Only the weight that is gone.
You cannot retrieve it. You cannot rewind time.You cannot claim it back with effort or speech.You are left with one thing: Your own emptiness.And it is terrifying.
Because standing here, you see it all:The pride that thought it owned what was lent.The negligence that allowed weight to slip.The subtle entitlement that made carelessness feel safe.Everything. Exposed.
No words, no motion, no plan can fix this.No memory, no reputation, no skill can restore what has gone under.Only mercy can.
So you stand. Shame burning in your chest.Fear trembling in your bones. Faith clinging by the smallest thread.And you do what the man before you did:You wail. You howl.You cry until the sound breaks through the disbelief.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.
And in that wail— something fragile appears:Hope. Mercy. The tree waiting in the water.
You cannot take it. You cannot earn it. You cannot command it.You can only receive it.
And standing at the water's edge,with nothing left but honesty and faith, you begin to understand:This is where the impossible begins.
Part X

The Cry That Breaks You

And here it comes— the cry that cannot be rehearsed.Not a prayer. Not a clever confession. Not a polished repentance.Just the raw, jagged sound of a soul recognizing the truth:
"It was borrowed."
It hits the chest like iron. It shatters the pride.It unmasks the pretense.It unmakes the illusions you carried like armor.
Because this cry does not ask for restoration.It does not bargain. It does not plead with heaven. It only says:
"I am empty.
I failed as a steward.
I cannot recover what was lent to me.
Alas, master!"
And that is holy terror.To see your own hands— hands that swung, served, advised, moved—and realize they were holding nothing.To recognize that others trusted you for what you no longer carried.To know that the river has taken more than iron.It has taken pretense. It has taken entitlement.It has swallowed the quiet pride that you thought made you strong.
And yet… Even as the cry breaks you, mercy lingers.The tree waits. The cross waits.The One who lent what sank is able to restore it in ways your hands cannot.
This is the cry that opens the heart to revival.Not because it deserves revival. Not because it earned it.But because God's mercy moves where the honest wail is heard.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.

And only when the cry pierces your soul—
only when you are undone in spirit—
can the mercy that lifts iron begin to work.
It is not comfort. It is not reassurance.It is the first step toward resurrection.
Part XI

When Iron Defies Nature

And then— if mercy moves— something impossible happens.The iron rises.Not because of your effort. Not because of your striving.Not because you finally "got it right."It rises because God acts.Because the tree casts its shadow over the river.Because the One who lent what sankdecides to restore it in ways that defy nature.
You may still be trembling.You may still feel the weight of shame.You may still wail from the edge of the water.But the impossible begins to lift.
Iron cannot float.Spiritual presence cannot persist without God's backing.Faithfulness cannot survive on memory alone.And yet, here it is:Rising. Returning. Restored by mercy, not by motion.
This is the mystery of stewardship:What was borrowed cannot be owned.What was lent cannot be manipulated.What was lost cannot be reclaimed by human effort.It rises only because God wills it.
And in that rising, the heart trembles.Because now you see it clearly:It was never yours to begin with.It is never yours to maintain.It can only be received— again and again— as a gift.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.

And only when the iron defies its nature,
only when the river cannot hold it down,
do we fully understand the mercy of God that lifts what we could not.
Praise Him.For what sinks by human failing can rise by divine power.For what we lost in negligence can return in unimaginable grace.
Part XII

Mercy That Lifts What Sank

And now we come to the final revelation: Mercy.Not earned. Not deserved. Not the reward of effort. But mercy.
The tree stands in the river. The cross stretches into the sky.The One who lent what sank moves in a way the world cannot understand.
What was lost— the edge, the presence, the weight— rises.Not because you were faithful enough.Not because you finally measured up.Not because your wailing convinced heaven.But because God is merciful.
And in that mercy, everything changes:The axe is no longer just an object.It becomes a symbol of restoration.Of faithfulness returned. Of stewardship renewed.
You stand at the river's edge,hands trembling, heart broken, soul exposed.And yet— iron rises. Presence returns. Faithfulness blooms again.
The cry that broke you—"Alas, master! It was borrowed!"—becomes the very thing that opens the door to mercy.
No effort can restore what was lost.No reputation can bring it back.No plan can lift it from the river bottom.Only mercy.
Everything we hold was borrowed.
Everything can be returned to us— not by striving, but by God alone.
Let this be your posture:Tremble at what is sacred. Guard what is entrusted.Wail honestly when you fail. And receive mercy when it comes.
For the river has done its work.The axe has sunk. And the tree has already entered the water.
Alas, master. It was borrowed.
And by God's mercy, it rises again.
✦ Closing

A Word on Stewardship

Tremble at What Is Sacred
Handle what is entrusted with holy fear. The weight you carry is not yours to own.
Guard What Is Entrusted
Vigilance is not optional. The slip begins in the moment you stop fearing the loss.
Wail Honestly When You Fail
The cry that is finally honest — "It was borrowed" — is the cry that opens the door to mercy.
Receive Mercy When It Comes
Iron rises not by effort, not by pride, not by pretense. But by God alone.
Alas, master. It was borrowed. And by God's mercy, it rises again.

❧ Sermon

Funds Held in Trust

Key Verse — 2 Kings 6:5

Mark ye well! Do ye ken what it is to hold that which is not thine? To clutch it tight, yet know it may vanish in a heartbeat?

The sons of the prophets huddled in their narrow rooms. Walls too close. Work too great. They went down to the Jordan, axes in hand, borrowed from one another. Not their own. Men of God, aye— but novices with iron. Hands made for prayer, not timber.

And then— it came to pass. The axe head slipped. It sank.

By my troth! The river brooks no dalliance. It swallowed it whole. No hand could reach it. No mortal wit could save it.

Shock. Shame. Helplessness. Heart hammering. Throat tight. Fain would one flee, but the bank gives no purchase.

And the cry tore from the man's soul:

"Alas, Master! For it was borrowed!"

Not I lost it. Not It fell by chance. But naked despair. Helplessness laid bare. Truth stripped before the Almighty.

Elisha demanded: "Where fell it?" The man pointed. No hiding. No pretense. God's mercy, or a pox upon pretense!

The prophet cast a stick. The iron rose. Not by skill. Not by mortal cunning. By mercy alone.

Steward! Mark me well!

All that thou holdest— capital, trust, influence— is borrowed. It is lent, not thine to keep. God discerned a spark of faithfulness. Not because of cleverness. Not because of worth.

The Jordan shall rise against thee. Suddenly. Silently. And thou shalt quake as if the river were thy judge.

Thy first act? Not defence. Not explanation. Not concealment.

"Alas, Master! It was borrowed!"

Confess fully. Point to the precise place of failure. Tremble. Shiver. Receive mercy, if it be granted, like a lifeline flung across the roaring flood.

Take it up again— Trembling. Aware. Reverent.

Thou art not master. Thou art steward. The river observes. The Master gazes. The pew trembles with thee.

Will ye answer honestly? Or cling to pride as the Jordan swallows thy testimony?

Every glance thou thought unseen. Every whisper imagined hidden. All observed. All accounted.

Deceive thyself no longer. The axe was borrowed. Thy stewardship is lent. And the Master shall call for account.

♩ Anthem

The Song of Stewardship — Eleven Verses

On the quiet weight of Christian financial stewardship — holding what is entrusted, absorbing risk without complaint, in the light of 2 Kings 6.

Alas, my master! 'twas but lent,The prophet's servant cried;So every sum we hold on earthIs borrowed, not our pride. Entrusted capital we keep,Not ours to grasp or claim;A weighty charge upon the hand,A stewardship of name. We guard it with unslacken'd care,We mark it in the book;Each movement weigh'd with prudent fear,Nor leave one farthing shook. Not swift increase, nor seasons keptBy promise bold or fair;But conscience clear before our God,And faithful, utmost care. The principal will pass away,The road hath turns and night;Yet man is never undone hereBy fortune's changing light, But by the trust he fails to keep,By slack or faithless hand;A charge of honour calls us allBefore the Judge to stand. No glittering show, no empty boast,No dazzling words we give;But steady prudence, word kept true,And hearts that purely live. If this seem slow, or wanting show,And tries the waiting mind,There are who deal in brighter arts—Let such go to their kind. For soon the sum and fund depart,Like tools for one brief day;But faithfulness and holy careShall never fade away. All that is lent remains but lent,And still that word abides;No gain can ease the borrower's heart,No reckon'd sum decides. Though mercy meet us by the stream,And loss be stay'd awhile,The cry returns— it was but lent—And stills the soul from guile.

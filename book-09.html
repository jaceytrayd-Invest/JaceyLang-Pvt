Though It Tarry

On the soul corrected by the answer it did not expect

A prophetic series in six chapters — drawn from Habakkuk 1–3 — on the prophet who brought his cause before God in certainty, and was corrected by the answer.

Prologue

The Burden He Carried

The burden did not come from without. It rose within him.
He saw — and could not unsee.
Violence in the streets. Strife without restraint. Judgment fallen slack.
He brought it before the Lord, as one persuaded he stood in truth.
O soul — there is a seeing that feels like righteousness,
yet has not passed through fire.
Chapter I

The Answer and the Protest

And the Lord answered.
Not with correction of the evil. But with something heavier.
“I work a work in your days — which ye will not believe.”
Then it was spoken: “I raise the Chaldeans.”
The answer did not resolve the burden. It deepened it.
O soul — God may answer thee,
and yet overturn all that thou hast understood.
He heard — and could not receive it.
“Thou art of purer eyes than to behold evil.”
He spoke what was true. He held to what he knew of God.
Shall the Holy employ the cruel? Shall judgment proceed through violence?
He stood upon truth — yet stood against the answer.
O soul — it is possible to speak rightly,
and yet resist what God is doing.
Chapter II

The Watch Set

The question did not depart. It returned upon him.
Not only — Why this? But — Who art thou to weigh it?
The matter shifted. From the wicked he saw — to the man who saw them.
O soul — God will not leave thee as observer;
He will bring thee into the matter itself.
So he withdrew. Not from the Lord — but from his own insistence.
“I will stand upon my watch.”
No more argument. No further pressing.
He stood — to hear what would be said, and what he must answer when reproved.
O soul — there is a standing where the soul waits
not to be justified, but to be corrected.
Chapter III

The Vision and the Wound of Delay

“Write the vision. Make it plain.”
It was given — clear, set, unaltered.
Yet it did not soothe him. It spoke of time. It exposed the heart. It divided men — the lifted, and the just.
O soul — the vision is not given to quiet thee,
but to lay thee bare.
“For the vision is yet for an appointed time.”
The word did not come near. It stretched away from him.
Though it would surely come — it would not come now.
“Though it tarry — wait.”
No sign was granted. No easing given. Only delay.
O soul — delay is the fire
wherein the soul is tried without relief.
“Behold, his soul which is lifted up is not upright in him.”

Not between nations — but within the heart.

“But the just shall live by his faith.”

Not by answer. Not by sight.
But by remaining — when nothing answers.

O soul — this is no comfort; it is a sentence laid upon thee.
Chapter IV

The Breaking Within

The fig tree would not blossom. The vine would fail. The labour would yield nothing. The stalls would stand empty.
What the vision declared would strip the land bare. The promise did not prevent it.
O soul — God may show thee the loss,
and still require thee to trust beyond it.
“When I heard — my belly trembled.”
His strength gave way. Rot entered his bones. His steps lost firmness.
This was not triumph. This was undoing.
Yet he did not depart.
O soul — true faith trembles under God,
yet refuses to flee.
Chapter V

The Turning and the Summons

Yet.
Not because the burden lifted. Not because the vision changed.
But because resistance failed.
“I will rejoice in the Lord.”
Not in the answer. Not in the outcome. In God alone.
O soul — faith is born
where the need to understand is surrendered.
“The Lord God is my strength.”
Not before the struggle. Not in the questioning. After the yielding.
He would walk — not on ground made level, but upon heights not chosen.
O soul — God strengthens not the resisting heart,
but the yielded one.
Wilt thou accept an answer that wounds thee?
Wilt thou remain when God overturns thy measure?
Wilt thou wait when the vision denies thy relief?

For the just do not live by resolution —
but by God Himself.
Chapter VI

Though It Tarry

T H O U G H   I T   T A R R Y
I brought my cause before the Lord, Persuaded I was right; He answered not by mending it, But deepened still the night.
The vision came with weight of time, No comfort in its frame; It showed me loss before it came, Yet bound me to the same.
My strength gave way, my spirit shook, No ground beneath my plea; Yet still I stood upon my watch — Till God had searched through me.
O soul, though truth delay thy peace,
And answers wound thy plea;
The just shall live by faith alone —
And God Himself shall be.
